# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Couples (dating, engaged, married, long-term partners) who want a private, low-friction place to keep wishlists for each other. Often used around birthdays, anniversaries, holidays, and Christmas — but works year-round as a running "things I'd love" list. They're iPhone users, comfortable with iCloud, and want the experience to feel personal, not like a shopping cart.

Secondary context: one partner is shopping for a gift and wants to see what the other wants without spoiling the surprise. The "claim" feature is the unlock — quietly mark an item as "I've got this" so the other person doesn't see it disappear.

## Product Purpose

Listmates is a paired wishlist app for couples. Two people link their phones once via iCloud, each keeps a private list of things they want, and they can see each other's lists. Items can be secretly claimed for gifting — surprise preserved. Built native with CloudKit, no accounts, no servers, no spam.

Success looks like a couple opening the app a few times a year — before a birthday, around the holidays, when something cool catches their eye — and feeling like the app got out of the way. It's not a daily-use product; it's a small, perfect ritual.

## Brand Personality

Warm, considered, intimate. Three words: **thoughtful, calm, romantic** — but never saccharine. Voice is quiet and confident, like a well-designed object. We never say "delight" or "magic." We talk about the small acts of paying attention that make a relationship feel known.

Emotional goals: the site should feel like the inside of a private notebook the two of them share, not a SaaS landing page. Restraint is the headline. Warmth is the texture.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing pages: hero metric, feature card grid, gradient text, testimonials carousel. None of that.
- Wedding registry sites (Zola, The Knot) — too transactional, too commerce-y, too pastel.
- Notion / Todoist clones — Listmates is not a productivity app and shouldn't read like one.
- Crypto/AI startup energy: neon, dark mode by default, "the future of X."
- Couple-y kitsch: hearts, cursive fonts, blush-and-gold weddings, stock photos of hands holding coffee.

## Design Principles

1. **Quiet by default, warm on touch.** The page rests in restraint. Color and motion appear when the user engages, not as decoration.
2. **The product is the proof.** Show real screens, real interactions, real copy from the app. Don't over-illustrate what's already beautiful in the app itself.
3. **Couple of one, not crowd of many.** Every word and image speaks to a single pair. No "millions of users," no testimonial walls, no logo bars.
4. **Native-first.** This is an iOS app; the site should feel like it was made by the same hand. SF Rounded, generous radii, deference to the device.
5. **Earn the scroll.** Each section has one idea, told plainly. If a section can be cut, cut it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA contrast minimum on all text, including over warm cream backgrounds and rose accents.
- Honor `prefers-reduced-motion`: scroll-driven and ambient motion collapse to fades or static.
- All interactive elements keyboard-reachable with visible focus rings on the rose accent.
- Inclusive language: "your partner," "the two of you" — never gendered, never assuming marriage.
- Imagery should not be visually exclusive (no single archetype of couple).
